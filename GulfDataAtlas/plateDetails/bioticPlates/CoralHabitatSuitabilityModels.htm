• Deep-Sea Corals
    Suitable Habitat

    Framework-forming deep-sea corals are an important living marine resource, providing three-dimensional habitat structure for fishes and invertebrates and forming oases of biodiversity. Unfortunately, their slow growth rates and vulnerability to disturbance pose challenges to their survival; and due to the logistical difficulty and expense of deep-sea exploration, much less is known about their distribution than that of their shallow-water counterparts. Predictive modeling of deep-sea coral habitat can provide this critical information which supports conservation efforts, management of offshore activities affecting the seafloor and guides future exploration. Habitat modeling also lends insights into the environmental factors driving the distribution of these corals, increasing our understanding of how these unique ecosystems function.

    Scientists from NOAA's National Centers for Coastal Ocean Science (NCCOS) and NOAA Fisheries have developed high-resolution, predictive habitat suitability models for framework-forming deep-sea corals. MaxEnt and ENMTools software were used to conduct a stepwise model selection on a presence-only coral location database and a suite of environmental and oceanographic predictor variables. The results of the model output shown here have calibrated thresholds that allow comparison across different coral groups. These models depict the predicted likelihood of suitable habitat for framework-forming deep-sea corals and the resulting maps can be used to identify potential locations of these corals (Kinlan et al., 2013).

    The Gulf of Mexico models shown here are some of the more significant coral taxa found in this region. Habitat suitability likelihood classes range from 0 (low likelihood of suitable coral habitat) to 10 (very high likelihood of suitable coral habitat). These models/maps are part of a larger NOAA NCCOS effort to model deep-sea coral habitat suitability across all U.S. coasts. The full range of deep-sea coral habitat modeling outputs, maps, and associated data are hosted by the Bureau of Ocean Energy Management (BOEM)-NOAA Marine Cadastre.

    Coral Taxonomic Groups:

    All Framework-Forming Corals
    This habitat suitability map includes all deep coral that can provide significant three-dimensional structure, and in this region includes stony corals (Scleractinia), black corals (Antipatharia), and gorgonians and certain other soft corals (Alcyonacea).

    All Framework-Forming Stony Corals (Scleractinia)
    Scleractinians are often the dominant reef-building corals in shallow environments and grow hard calcium carbonate skeletons in a wide variety of formations. The deep-sea scleractinians that provide major structure in the Gulf of Mexico and are modeled here include Lophelia spp., Madrepora spp., Madracis spp., Solenosmilia variabilis, and Enallopsammia spp.

    Lophelia pertusa
    Lophelia is the most abundant colonial scleractinian in deep waters (>100 m) in the Gulf of Mexico, and forms extensive branching thickets in some regions. Due to its significant role in the region, it is also mapped separately from other framework-forming species.

    Black Corals (Antipatharia)
    At least 20 antipatharian species are found in the Gulf of Mexico, and all potentially framework-forming species are included here. Some, such as Leiopathes spp., can form extensive bushy colonies with thorny skeletons made of hard protein.

    Gorgonians (Alcyonacea)
    Alcyonacea encompasses several different groups of soft corals with flexible skeletons made of a variety of substances. Gorgonians, also known as sea fans and sea whips, are a subgroup of Alcyonacea modeled here. Common deep-sea examples in the Gulf of Mexico include Callogorgia spp., Paragorgia spp., Paramuricea spp., Sibobagorgia spp. (bubblegum coral), and Acanella arbuscula (bamboo coral).
