Mangrove ecosystems in Florida

Mangrove ecosystems occur in dense, brackish swamps along coastal intertidal shorelines of the Florida peninsula.  The Florida mangrove community includes three true mangrove species— Red Mangrove (Rhizophora mangle), Black Mangrove (Avicennia germinans) and White Mangrove (Laguncularia racemosa)—and one associated species, Buttonwood (Conocarpus erectus). These plant species have adapted to specialized salinity and inundation conditions and generally grow in overlapping zones along the shoreline. Red Mangroves are easily recognized by their dense network of prop roots and their ability to grow in open water. Black Mangroves grow at slightly higher elevations and do not have prop roots. These plants are characterized by their many pneumatophores that grow up from the roots and poke out of the soil above the water level. White Mangroves grow at the highest elevations of the three Florida mangrove species and may have pneumatophores, depending on the conditions where they are growing. While Buttonwood is a salt-tolerant plant, it is generally found in upland areas along the fringe of Florida’s mangrove ecosystems. 

Mangrove ecosystems are distributed from Cedar Key on the Gulf of Mexico to Ponce de Leon Inlet on the Atlantic Ocean, and cover an area of between 450,000 to 550,000 acres in Florida. However, since mangroves are a tropical species and Florida has a subtropical climate, nearly 90% of all mangroves are found in southernmost Florida (Collier, Lee, Miami-Dade, and Monroe Counties).  Therefore, climate and temperature are the major factors that control the distribution of mangroves in Florida. Other factors that influence the composition, distribution, and extent of mangrove communities in Florida include salinity, hydrology, tides, sea level, nutrient runoff, and wave energy.  Although mangroves are protected by local and state laws, mangrove loss from human impacts continues to reduce the abundance of Florida’s mangrove ecosystems.  Some large urban bay and estuary systems have seen major losses over the past century, including Tampa Bay (44% reduction), Charlotte Harbor (59% reduction), Lake Worth (87% reduction), and Indian River Lagoon (86% loss of functional ecosystems).   

Mangrove forests are among the world’s most biologically productive ecosystems, and they play an important role in improving water quality by accumulating excess nutrients, heavy metals, and trace elements. Mangroves also provide critical habitat and protected nursery areas for numerous commercially important marine species, including snook, snapper, tarpon, jack, sheepshead, red drum, spiny lobster, oysters, and shrimp. By some estimates, 75% of game fish and 90% of commercial fish depend on mangrove habitats at some point during their life cycles.  Therefore, mangroves are of great importance to Florida’s booming recreational and commercial fishing industries, and both would likely exhibit drastic decline without healthy mangrove forests.  In addition to marine species, many birds, reptiles, amphibians, and mammals rely on mangroves as important habitat above the water line. Mangrove branches serve as important rookeries and roosting areas for some of Florida’s most iconic coastal birds, including brown pelicans, roseate spoonbills, frigatebirds, osprey, egrets, and herons.
