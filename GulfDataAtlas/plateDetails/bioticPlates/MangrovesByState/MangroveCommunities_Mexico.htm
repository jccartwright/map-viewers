Mangroves of Mexico

The mangrove is a dense tree or shrub-like formation of woody vegetation from 1 to 30 m in height. It is composed of one or more species of mangrove and contains few herbaceous or climbing species. The species that make up the mangrove have perennial, slightly succulent leaves with entire edges. In Mexico, four mangrove species predominate (Rhizophora mangle, Laguncularia racemosa, Avicennia germinans and Conocarpus erectus). These species may form vegetative associations or be found in mono-specific forests.

Coastal wetlands, especially mangroves, provide a wide variety of environmental services: they are sites of nourishment, refuge, and growth of juvenile crustacean and fish. As such, they not only sustain a large part of fishery production, but also are used as a fuel (firewood), have a high aesthetic and recreational value, act as a natural flood control mechanism and as barriers against hurricanes and saline intrusion, control erosion, and protect the coasts. They also improve water quality by functioning as a biological filter, contribute to the maintenance of such natural processes as responses to sea-level change, maintain sedimentation processes, and serve as a refuge for wild flora and fauna, among other functions.

In spite of their importance, the worldwide coverage of mangroves has decreased considerably. It is estimated that approximately 35% of mangroves worldwide have been lost in the past two decades.[1] In Mexico, they are mainly affected by tree felling and removal to clear space for agriculture, livestock production, fisheries, and tourism.

At the global level, Mexico is among those countries with the greatest mangrove coverage, although some discrepancy exists between reported estimates. Because there are no reliable estimates of rates of change in Mexico’s mangroves, and because causal factors of these changes have not been evaluated at the national scale, CONABIO initiated a program of monitoring of the mangroves of Mexico several years ago. The goal was to generate the knowledge with which to influence public policy for improved planning and management of this ecosystem at the national scale.

For the national inventory of the year 2005, the estimated coverage of mangroves in Mexico is 774090 hectares. The Yucatan Peninsula (states of Campeche, Yucatan, and Quintana Roo) ranks number one in mangrove extension with 54.5% (421573 ha) of the total mangroves of the country, and the Gulf of Mexico region (states of Tamaulipas, Veracruz, and Tabasco) sums up to 11.3% (87474 ha). The area of mangroves found within Federal and State Protected Natural Areas (2005) is 54% (2005). Nationwide, 81 mangrove sites were identified as being of biological importance and in immediate need of ecological rehabilitation.
