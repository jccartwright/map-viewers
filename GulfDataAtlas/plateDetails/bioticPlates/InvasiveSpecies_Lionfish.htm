• Nonindigenous Aquatic Species
    Lionfish
    Pterois volitans, Pterois miles

    Red lionfish (Pterois volitans) is a species native to the western Pacific Ocean from southern Japan to Australia and eastward to French Polynesia1. The devil firefish's (Pterois miles) native range includes the Red Sea and Indian Ocean, south to Sumatra (Schofield, 2009). Both species became established along the US Atlantic Coast, Caribbean Sea, and the Gulf of Mexico during the past decade, and as they are difficult to distinguish from each other visually in their non-native populations, both are generally identified as "lionfish" (Schofield et al., 2013). Lionfish are found in a range of habitat types including natural and artificial reefs, bridge pilings, and seagrass beds in depths up to 300 m (Schofield et al., 2013). These fishes can grow to over 20 inches during a lifespan of more than 15 years and possess venomous spines that can cause injury to humans and predators. They are voracious predators themselves, consuming over 56 species of commercially and ecologically important fishes and many invertebrates, and are able to out-compete less destructive native predators (Lionfish QuickFacts ).

    Lionfish were likely introduced into US coastal waters as unwanted pet fish released from home aquariums into the ocean (Schofield et al., 2013). They are the first marine fishes to invade the region and by 2002, lionfish were established from Miami to North Carolina, and throughout the Florida Keys, Yucatan, and Caribbean by 2009 (Schofield, 2010). From 2010 to present, established populations spread throughout the Gulf of Mexico. The first lionfish in the Gulf was found already dead during a toxic Karenia brevis bloom in 2006 off the coast of Treasure Island, Florida. The specimen showed only a minimal brevitoxin exposure when tested, indicating that the fish had only been in Gulf waters a short time and likely discarded from an aquarium. This sample is therefore considered an anomaly and not a precursor to the current population (Schofield, 2009). Although it was thought that their intolerance of cold temperatures would limit expansion of the invaded range, lionfish have been found off the southern coast of Long Island, New York in 56°F waters (Schofield et al., 2013) (see animated time-series map of the USGS-NAS data points).

    This map shows only the observational sightings of lionfish within the Gulf of Mexico, a subset of the larger US Geological Survey Nonindigenous Aquatic Species (USGS-NAS) lionfish database, so although lionfish are also established throughout Cuba and the Caribbean and Atlantic coastlines, those points are not shown here (see the USGS-NAS database lionfish map viewer for the map of the complete database). These data points are compiled from publications and reports to the USGS-NAS database and are reviewed for reliability. They are opportunistic reports, not the results of a systematic sampling program, and therefore represent occurrence but not species abundance. Lionfish sightings can be reported to the USGS-NAS via an online form . The metadata record for the USGS-NAS database contains additional details for review and QA/QC procedures. The map shown here uses different colors to represent the dates of the observations and different symbols to represent whether the point is accurate (GPS field measurements were provided) or approximate (determined by a location's description).
