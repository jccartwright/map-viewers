• Mobile Bay
    Submerged Aquatic Vegetation Habitat Suitability

    Mobile Bay, Alabama, is an inlet of the Northern Gulf of Mexico. From the 1940s until 2002, seagrass extent has declined throughout the bay and adjacent estuaries. This study explores the role of land-cover/land-use (LCLU) change and climate change in watersheds adjacent to shallow-water ecosystems in coastal Alabama. Changing conditions in the watersheds lead to changes in discharge flows and associated changes in water properties and submerged aquatic vegetation (SAV) habitat throughout the estuary. Watershed and hydrodynamic modeling were performed to evaluate the impact of LCLU change and climate change in watersheds adjacent to the estuary on shallow aquatic ecosystems.

    The Prescott Spatial Growth Model (PSGM) was used to generate future scenarios based on existing growth trends. A paper map of LCLU in 1948 was used as a baseline to represent a "green" pre-development scenario. Since 1948, Mobile and Baldwin counties have become more urbanized. The increased urban land cover is replacing forest, agriculture, and pasture land, and is most prominent in the southern and western areas that surround the city of Mobile, the shoreline areas on the bay's eastern shore, and the beach areas of Gulf Shores and Orange Beach, Alabama. Remotely sensed Landsat-derived National Land Cover Data (NLCD) were used as the source for LCLU maps for 1992 and 2001. These two maps were then used as the "current" scenario inputs for the PSGM model to generate a future LCLU map for the year 2030.

    Watershed and hydrodynamic model outputs were linked to separately evaluate the impacts of these LCLU changes. Data for future changes in temperature, precipitation, and sea level rise were provided by the Intergovernmental Panel on Climate Change (IPCC) and used to create the climate scenarios for the watershed and hydrodynamic modeling (2005, 2025, and 2050).

    Overall, LCLU changes are increasing freshwater flows into Mobile Bay and are altering temperature, salinity, and total suspended solids (TSS). Increasing urbanization by replacing agriculture/pasture land reduces TSS in the water column. Conversely, increasing urbanization or agricultural/pasture land by replacing forest land results in larger TSS levels. Higher sediment loads normally were strongly correlated with higher TSS levels, except in areas with a large extent of wetlands. LCLU changes are having a greater influence on sediment loads being discharged into the estuary, and climate changes are projected to have a more significant effect on salinity gradients in the estuary.

    The changes in sediment concentrations and their associated impact on light attenuation in the aquatic ecosystem were used to perform an ecological analysis to evaluate the impact on SAV habitat for the LCLU and climate scenarios. Sediment changes are particularly important, since the amount of TSS significantly influences light attenuation, a critical factor in the health of submerged aquatic vegetation. GIS habitat suitability maps for each scenario were developed. This is the key product benefiting the Mobile Bay coastal environmental managers that integrates the influences of temperature, salinity, and sediment due to LCLU and climate-driven flow changes with the restoration potential of SAVs.
