• Submerged Aquatic Vegetation
    Gulf Islands - Mississippi District

    Mapping areas of seagrass is crucial, in part because the extent of seagrass habitat can serve as a general indicator of coastal ecosystem health (Green and Short, 2003; Mumby et al., 1997). Federally designated as Essential Fisheries Habitat, seagrasses provide nurseries for juvenile finfish and shellfish and are an important food source for many marine species and wading birds. They also increase water clarity through sediment stabilization, increase nutrient uptake and sequestration, as well as increase dissolved oxygen while reducing atmospheric carbon dioxide via photosynthesis.

    These Mississippi barrier islands were impacted dramatically by Hurricane Katrina on August 29, 2005.

    To determine potential impacts of the storm on island seagrass populations, seagrass maps were developed using historical digitized, aerial true-color photography acquired on October 15, 2003 (0.3-m resolution). The photography was compared with digital multispectral imagery acquired on October 29, 2006 (1-m resolution). Seagrass areal coverage and distribution were quantified via digital image processing and visual image interpretation. Results were compared between the two dates to determine post-Katrina change. Assuming that seagrass coverage immediately before Katrina was similar to that determined for 2003, the generally greater coverage in 2006 suggests that the storm impact served to stimulate seagrass growth (Carter et al., 2009).
