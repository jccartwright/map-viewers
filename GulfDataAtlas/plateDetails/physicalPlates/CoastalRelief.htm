    Modeling and mapping of coastal processes (e.g., tsunamis, hurricane storm surge, sea-level rise, contaminant dispersal, etc.) require digital representations of Earth's solid surface. These representations are referred to as digital elevation models (DEMs).

    NOAA's National Centers for Environmental Information (NCEI) builds and distributes these high-resolution DEMs that integrate ocean bathymetry, land topography, and shoreline data obtained from various sources (federal, state, and local government agencies, academic institutions, and private companies). DEMs are also used for ecosystems management and habitat research, coastal and marine spatial planning, and hazard mitigation and community preparedness. They also help visualize the coastal zone.1

    1NCEI's DEMs are referenced to a vertical tidal datum of North American Vertical Datum of 1988 (NAVD 88) or to mean high water (MHW) and horizontal datum of either North American Datum of 1983 (NAD 83) or World Geodetic System of 1984 (WGS 84). Cell size ranges from 1/3 arc-second (~10 m) to 1 arc-second (~30 m).
