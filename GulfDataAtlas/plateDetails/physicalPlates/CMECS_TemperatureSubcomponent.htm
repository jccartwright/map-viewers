• Coastal and Marine Ecological Classification Standard (CMECS) Application for
    Sea Surface Temperature

    These maps present 5-year seasonal means, as estimated from MODIS/Aqua satellite imagery, of sea surface temperature in the northern Gulf of Mexico for fall, winter, spring, and summer1. Temperature has a considerable impact on photosynthesis, growth, metabolism, and mobility of organisms. Rates of microbial processes of decomposition, nitrogen fixation, and denitrification generally double with each increase of 10 °C. Organisms tolerate a particular temperature range, and the temperature optimum range may span only a few degrees. The solubility of oxygen and other gases is also temperature dependent.

    The Naval Research Laboratory at the Stennis Space Center (NRL/SSC) processed MODIS-Aqua satellite imagery covering the Gulf of Mexico, for a 5-year period from January 2005 to December 2009. Daily, level-1B image files were processed through the NRL/SSC Automated Processing System (APS). Six regions were processed at 1-km spatial resolution: western Gulf of Mexico, central Gulf of Mexico, eastern Gulf of Mexico, Mobile Bay, Tampa Bay, and Galveston Bay. Monthly composite images were generated from the daily scenes to reduce the effects of cloud cover. Sea surface temperature from MODIS-Aqua was calculated with the standard NASA processing algorithm; uncertainties are about 0.1 °C or less (Minnett et al., 2004).

    The maps were produced as part of a NASA-funded project to evaluate the classifiers and modifiers identified in the Coastal and Marine Ecological Classification Standard (CMECS) Water Column Component. The CMECS is a catalog of terms that provides a means for classifying ecological units using a simple standard format and common terminology. CMECS offers a way to organize and interpret data about the marine environment, and it provides a common platform for interrelating data. It builds upon approaches from published national, regional, and local habitat classification procedures, and it offers an umbrella under which a national coastal and marine ecological classification can grow and evolve. The project was modeled after an exercise undertaken in the United Kingdom to characterize pelagic habitats in which sea surface temperature and salinity, as well as frontal probability, were used to delineate unique pelagic zones (Connor et al., 2006).

    CMECS temperature categories are established in intervals of sufficient range and resolution to provide meaningful ecological differences yet yield a parsimonious number of categories. Temperature categories are based on the British Columbia Marine Ecological Classification for Canada (Howes et al., 1994, Zacharias et al., 1998; Resource Information Standards Committee 2002), which was modified to add the higher temperature ranges typical of the subtropics and tropics. In this study, temperature ranges were in 10°C increments. In the Federal Geographic Data Committee (FGDC) endorsed CMECS, temperature ranges have been further refined and occur in 5°C increments.
