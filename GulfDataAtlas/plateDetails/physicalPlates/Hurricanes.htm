• Tropical Storm-Hurricane Activity
    1851 to 2012

    Hurricanes are major storm systems that can have significant impact on the Gulf of Mexico region. Storms may bring torrential rain, damaging winds, storm surge, and tornadoes to coastal and inland areas. A storm's intensity is classified using the Saffir-Simpson scale which is based on wind speeds. Although a storm is not called a "hurricane" until it reaches Category 1 status, a storm will be given a name when it reaches tropical storm status in order to simplify communications of forecasts and storm warnings.

    The data used in these graphics are from the International Best Track Archive for Climate Stewardship (IBTrACS), which is a synthesis of all global tropical cyclone track and intensity databases. More information is available from NOAA's National Centers for Environmental Information (NCEI).

    The Gulf of Mexico tropical cyclones is a subset of the North Atlantic hurricane database, or HURDAT, from 1842 (also known as besttrack). The original HURDAT contains six hourly storm center location (closest 0.1 degrees latitude and longitude), estimated maximum 1-minute sustained winds at 33-ft heights, and when available, central pressure. It should be noted the original HURDAT format has been retired, superseded by HURDAT2. More information is available from NOAA's Atlantic Oceanographic and Meteorological Laboratory (AOML). In addition, these fields tend to be smoothed and do not represent the storm center wobbles and slight storm speed changes between the six-hour periods of 00, 06, 12, and 18 UTC.

    The products shown herein present climatology information on tropical storm activity. For example, one can view how often a tropical cyclone has passed within a given location, providing a "density count." For analysis, a 10-minute grid was applied to the storm data to show their density. These can be partitioned into storm intensity classes as well, including tropical storm and Saffir-Simpson scale categories.
