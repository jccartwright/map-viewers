• Seawater Temperature

    Seawater temperature is a critical environmental indicator. In situ measurements obtained from instruments carried aboard oceanographic research and merchant vessels are the main source of subsurface temperature information. Temperature data distribution from the World Ocean Database for the Gulf of Mexico shows good overall coverage: measurements increase toward the northeast and decrease to the southwest. Climatological mean fields of temperature for each season on a 0.10° grid were calculated from these measurements.

    Temperatures at 10-m depths for winter, spring, summer, and fall1 show the effects of surface forcing, mainly from the annual cycle of solar radiation. Deeper in the water column, temperatures at 250 m for winter, spring, summer, and fall show much less variability from direct surface forcing. These temperatures also show a structure defined more by the warmer Loop Current Waters entering between the Yucatan Peninsula and Cuba and then meeting the cooler Gulf of Mexico waters.2

    1For these maps, winter = January - March; spring = April - June; summer = July - September; fall = October - December. The maps are based on the objectively analyzed mean climatological values from NCEI's climatology page.

    2Climatological mean temperature data for the four seasons at 102 standard levels from the surface to 5500-m depths for the Gulf of Mexico and environs are available at the NCEI's regional climatology page along with further information and figures for all standard depths. These climatologies are based on data collected from January 21, 1864 to December 31, 2010.
