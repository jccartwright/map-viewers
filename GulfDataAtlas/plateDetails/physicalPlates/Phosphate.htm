• Nutrients - Phosphate

    Dissolved inorganic nutrients in seawater, such as phosphate, are essential for sustaining marine biological production. Their concentrations are often measured by means of photometric analysis of discrete and underway water samples (e.g., Gordon et al., 1993). The spatial and vertical distribution of phosphate is dominated by biochemical and physical circulation processes. The northern Gulf of Mexico includes intense "dead zones" or hypoxic waters that develop usually in the summer near the mouth of the Mississippi River. Hypoxic waters develop because of eutrophication caused by seasonal nutrient enrichment from the Mississippi-Atchafalaya River Basin, followed by decomposition of organic matter and associated oxygen consumption.

    The spatial and temporal coverage of phosphate data in the Gulf of Mexico is not uniform: most of the historical data were collected over the continental shelf near shallow intertidal areas (<200 m depth). From these measurements, climatological mean fields of phosphate on a one-degree grid were calculated from quality-controlled data in the World Ocean Database 2013 (Garcia et al., 2009). Phosphate at 10 m depths for winter, spring, summer, and fall1 show relatively large horizontal gradients due to the effects of surface biological uptake, physical forcing (e.g., vertical stratification, upwelling of nutrient-rich water, and circulation), river nutrient inputs, and decomposition of labile organic matter. Deeper in the water column, phosphate at 250 m for winter, spring, summer, and fall shows marked west-to-east zonal gradients, which reflect advection of waters of different preformed concentrations entering the Gulf of Mexico between the Yucatan Peninsula, Cuba, and Florida.

    1For these maps, winter = January - March; spring = April - June; summer = July - September; fall = October - December. The maps are based on the objectively analyzed mean climatological values from NCEI's climatology page. These climatologies are based on data collected from January 21, 1864 to December 31, 2010.
