• Gulf of Mexico Seafloor
    Geomorphology

    Seafloor geomorphology describes the bathymetric features and physical habitats of the ocean environment. The Blue Habitats global digital geomorphic seafloor features map (GSFM) (Harris et al., 2014) identifies these features by applying the International Hydrographic Organization (IHO) Standardization of Undersea Feature Names (version 2008) definitions and nomenclature to a GIS analysis of bathymetry datasets. A modified 30 arc-second SRTM30_PLUS global bathymetry grid (Becker et al, 2009) is the primary data source for the global map. Additional bathymetry data were used in the oceans surrounding the Arctic, and ancillary datasets such as sediment thickness, ocean crust age and existing geomorphology maps were used to verify results of the GIS analysis (Harris et al., 2014). New summary statistics, such as the number and area of features, and global and regional occurrence were also generated from this dataset (Harris et al., 2014).

    For this subset of the global GSFM, only the features that fall within the bounding box covering the Gulf of Mexico and the north-western Caribbean Sea are included. These features are organized in a hierarchical classification of GIS layers of increasing detail and specificity. The four base layer features, shelf, slope, abyss and hadal, broadly define the oceanic environment from the coast to its deepest depths and can be overlain by the classification and geomorphic features layers (Harris et al., 2014). The shelf and abyssal classification layers refine the base layers into low, medium, and high relief shelves, and abyssal plains, hills and mountains as determined by seafloor roughness; the individual geomorphic feature layers in the Gulf of Mexico are shelf valleys, escarpments, basins, terraces, canyons, rises, seamounts, fans, ridges, and plateaus. Bridges, sills, rift valleys, spreading ridges, and trenches are also found in the Caribbean Sea (Harris et al., 2014).
