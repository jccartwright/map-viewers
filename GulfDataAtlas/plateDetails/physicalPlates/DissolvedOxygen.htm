• Dissolved Oxygen

    Dissolved oxygen plays a critical role in most oceanic biogeochemical processes and is essential for most living resources. In the past few decades, dissolved O2 concentration measured by chemical methods is often analyzed following various modifications of the Winkler titration followed by end-detections by visual, amperometric, or photometric methods (e.g., Carpenter, 1965; Culberson and Huang, 1987; Knapp et al., 1990; Culberson et al., 1991; Dickson, 1994). Scientifically quality-controlled historical in situ dissolved oxygen data in the World Ocean Database 2013 obtained by chemical methods were used to construct annual, seasonal, and monthly climatological distribution fields of dissolved oxygen, apparent oxygen utilization (AOU), and dissolved oxygen saturation at selected standard depth levels of the world ocean on a one-degree latitude-longitude grid (Boyer et al., 2009; Garcia et al., 2010).

    Most of the historical O2 data were obtained from oceanographic research cruises occupying a number of selected oceanographic station locations. Several workers have reviewed the general distribution of water masses within the Gulf of Mexico (e.g., Rivas et al., 2005; Nowlin et al., 2001). In recent years, attention has been paid to oxygen hypoxia processes and the influence of the Mississippi River on the Louisiana Continental Shelf (e.g., Rabalais et al., 2007).

    Dissolved oxygen data distribution from the World Ocean Database for the Gulf of Mexico shows good overall coverage particularly over the shelf area. From these measurements, climatological mean fields of dissolved oxygen for each season on a one degree grid have been calculated (Garcia et al., 2010). At 10 meters depth, dissolved oxygen for winter, spring, summer, and fall1 are presented in these series of maps. At 250 meters depth, dissolved oxygen for winter, spring, summer, and fall are presented in these series of maps as examples.

    1For these maps, winter = January - March; spring = April - June; summer = July - September; fall = October - December. The maps are based on the objectively analyzed mean climatological values from NCEI's climatology page. These climatologies are based on data collected from January 21, 1864 to December 31, 2010.
