• Seawater Salinity

    Salinity is a key environmental indicator. The salinity of the ocean is not constant. Because the ocean is affected by a variety of such conditions as rain, river inflow, wind and ocean currents, the salinity in the Gulf of Mexico is widely variable and also varies with the seasons.

    In situ measurements obtained from instruments carried aboard oceanographic research ships are the main source of surface and subsurface salinity information. Salinity data distribution from the World Ocean Database for the Gulf of Mexico shows good resolution in the northeast and along most of the U.S. coast, where measurements decrease markedly to the west and the south. Climatological mean fields of salinity for each season on a 0.10° degree grid were calculated from these measurements.

    Salinity at depths of 10 m for winter, spring, summer, and fall1 shows the changing seasonal effect of the movement of waters from the Mississippi Outflow and other coastal waters. Deeper in the water column, the salinity at 250 m for winter, spring, summer, and fall shows much less variability from direct surface forcing, as well as a structure defined more by the saltier Loop Current Waters that enter between the Yucatan Peninsula and Cuba and then meet the relatively fresher Gulf of Mexico waters.2

    1For these maps, winter = January - March; spring = April - June; summer = July - September; fall = October - December. The maps are based on the objectively analyzed mean climatological values from NCEI's climatology page.

    2Climatological mean salinity data for the four seasons at 102 standard levels from the surface to 5500-m depths for the Gulf of Mexico and environs are available at the NCEI's regional climatology page along with further information and figures for all standard depths. These climatologies are based on data collected from January 21, 1864 to June 29, 2010.
