• Red Grouper
    Epinephelus morio

    Red grouper (Epinephelus morio) are a popular commercial fish that ranges in the Gulf of Mexico. Unlike the South Atlantic population, the Gulf red grouper population is healthy and is harvested at sustainable levels now that it has rebuilt after being declared overfished in the early 2000s.

    Red grouper are most commonly found around ledges, crevices, and caverns of rocky limestone reefs, and also near lower profile, live-bottom areas in water depths of 3 to 12 m. Red grouper are protogynous hermaphrodites, meaning they function as a female first and some later transition to males. Most females transform to males between the ages of 7 and 15 years. The adult red grouper can grow up to 49 inches in total length and weigh over 50 pounds. Their maximum age is 26 years. (NOAA's FishWatch - Red Grouper)

    Fisheries-Independent Data
    Federal

    This map shows the red grouper data from the Southeast Area Monitoring and Assessment Program (SEAMAP) and National Marine Fisheries Service (NMFS) fishery-independent Reef Fish and Bottom Longline resource surveys throughout the U.S. Gulf of Mexico. These are primarily adult fish but may represent some juveniles as well.

    Red grouper (primarily adult) relative abundance and sampling effort by 10-minute longitude by latitude blocks are summarized from 4,125 video camera sets taken during SEAMAP Reef Fish surveys from 1993 to 2009. Relative abundance is expressed as minimum count and sampling effort as the number of camera sets.

    Red grouper (primarily adult) relative abundance and sampling effort by 15-minute longitude by latitude blocks are summarized from 1,741 bottom longline sets taken during NMFS Bottom Longline surveys from 2000 to 2009. Relative abundance is expressed in catch per unit effort (CPUE) which represents the number caught per 1-hour tow. Sampling effort represents the number of bottom longline sets.

    Fisheries-Dependent Data
    Commercial Catch
    Bottom Longline

    The Gulf of Mexico reef fishery consists of approximately 821 federally permitted vessels as of 2013 (SERO). Primary gears used include bottom longline and vertical line (bandit or handline). Although many reef fish species are retained, the predominant target species are groupers, Epinephelus spp., and snappers, Lutjanus spp. Fishery data are collected by mandatory observer coverage, and characteristics of this year-round fishery and sampling protocol for the Gulf of Mexico reef fish observer program are described by Scott-Denton et al. (2011). The Magnuson-Stevens Fishery Conservation and Management Act (S. 2012--109th Congress, 2005) gives NOAA authority to place observers on U.S. fishing vessels, and also requires that NOAA Fisheries maintains the confidentiality of any information obtained through compliance with it.

    This map depicts a 'hot spot' analysis of red grouper catch-per-unit-effort (CPUE) based on observer coverage of the U.S. Gulf of Mexico bottom longline fishery from 2007 through 2013 (7,201 sets made during 272 trips). For confidentiality purposes, data were aggregated into 15-minute longitude by latitude blocks and the average CPUE per block was calculated. If any blocks contained data from less than 3 vessels, they were removed to protect confidentiality, while not adversely affecting the results of the analysis. The 'hot spot', or Getis-Ord Gi* statistic, is used to detect clusters of high or low catch CPUE values. A statistically significant Z-score results if the sum for a block and its neighbors, when compared proportionally to the sum of all blocks in the grid, is different than what is expected. A high positive Z-score indicates a hot spot where a block with high CPUE is surrounded by neighbors with similiarly high CPUE values. A low negative Z-score indicates a cold spot, where a low-CPUE block is surrounded by blocks with similarly low values. Areas that indicate no concentration of high or low CPUE values, i.e. blocks having dissimilar values or values near the mean for the entire grid, have Z-scores near 0.
