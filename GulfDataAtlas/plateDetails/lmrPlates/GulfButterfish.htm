• Gulf Butterfish
    Peprilus burti

    Gulf butterfish (Peprilus burti), which range in the Gulf of Mexico from the western Yucatan Peninsula to Tampa Bay, Florida, are most abundant in the northeast Gulf. They form large, loose schools across the continental shelf over sand and mud bottoms. Their depth ranges from 2 to 275 m, but they are most abundant from 155 to 225 m. Gulf butterfish are found near the bottom during the day and migrate up into the water column at night. Juveniles are often found under floating weeds or associated with jellyfish. Spawning occurs from September to October and late January to May in offshore areas.

    Gulf butterfish mature within 1 year and rarely live past 2 years, growing to a maxiumum of 10 inches (total length). Adults feed on jellyfish, small fish, crustaceans, and worms, while juveniles eat plankton and jellyfish. (Texas Parks & Wildlife Department, The Living Marine Resources of the Western Central Atlantic)  

    Fisheries-Independent Data
    Federal

    Relative abundance and sampling effort for Gulf butterfish are summarized in 10-minute longitude by latitude blocks from 11,637 40-ft trawls taken during the SEAMAP Summer and Fall Shrimp/Groundfish surveys from 1987 to 2009. Relative abundance is expressed in catch per unit effort (CPUE), which represents the number caught per 1-hour tow. Sampling effort represents the number of trawls that participated. The CPUE consisted of primarily adult fish, but may represent some juveniles as well.
