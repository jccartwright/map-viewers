• Eastern Oyster
    Crassostrea virginica Ostion americano

    The Eastern oyster (Crassostrea virginica Ostion Americano), a bivalve mollusc of the family Ostreidae, is found in the western Atlantic from the Gulf of St. Lawrence to the Yucatan Peninsula. In the Gulf of Mexico, this species occurs throughout estuaries, shallow nearshore waters, and on reefs located near river mouths. This species is commercially harvested throughout its range for its meat, the seasonality, largely based on water quality, for harvesting being state regulated. The species is also harvested recreationally. The fishery for the species is the fourth largest in the U.S. Gulf of Mexico and there is also a commercial fishery in Mexican waters. The Eastern oyster is not a Council-managed fishery species; however, NOAA, along with other federal agencies, contributes resource protection recommendations under permitting-requiring activities through the Clean Water Act or Rivers and Harbors Act.

    Populations of the Eastern oyster are found in the large estuarine bays and sounds of the Gulf with most concentrations found in waters <10 m deep. Sedentary in character, this benthic invertebrate attaches in clusters to shell reefs, firm mud/shell bottoms and other hard substrates. Spawning also occurs in estuarine habitats. In the Gulf of Mexico, April to October are the most active months, although spawning has been reported during all months except February and March. Nursery areas for pelagic larvae and settled juveniles are found in estuarine waters.

    The Eastern oyster filter-feeds principally on small unicellular algae and incidentally on suspended detrital particles. It is found in association with numerous predators, commensals, and parasites. Its planktonic larvae drift with the tides and currents until they settle on solid substrate.

    Oysters are particularly vulnerable to microbial pollution because they may also bioaccumulate bacteria from pollution sources, which may lead to closure of shellfish harvest areas due to high levels of fecal coliform bacteria in surface waters (>14 fc bacteria /100 mL). This source of pollution is one of the leading causes of water quality impairments in the coastal waters of the Gulf of Mexico.
