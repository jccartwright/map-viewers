• Gray Triggerfish
    Balistes capriscus

    The gray triggerfish (Balistes capriscus) is found throughout the Gulf of Mexico. Information is sparse, particularly for the early life stages. Adults are found offshore in waters between 10 m and 55 m, where they are associated with natural and artificial reefs. Spawning occurs in late spring and summer on offshore reef or hard-bottom habitats. They produce demersal eggs, which they deposit into nests and guard until hatching. Larvae and postlarvae are pelagic and are usually associated with Sargassum and other flotsam. Early and late juveniles are also associated with Sargassum and other flotsam, and can sometimes be found in mangrove estuaries. Triggerfish leave the surface Sargassum habitat in the fall, when juvenile fish move to reef habitat on the bottom.

    Gray triggerfish reach sexual maturity at 2 years of age, and can live up to 13 years. They can grow over 23 inches long (total length) and weigh up to 13 pounds. Adults feed on benthic invertebrates such as urchins, sea stars, bivalves, and barnacles. (South Atlantic Fishery Management Council, Florida Museum of Natural History, Final Environmental Impact Statement for the Generic Essential Fish Habitat Amendment)  

    Fisheries-Independent Data
    Federal

    For gray triggerfish, relative abundance and sampling effort in 10-minute longitude by latitude blocks are summarized from 11,637 40-ft trawls taken during the SEAMAP Summer and Fall Shrimp/Groundfish surveys from 1987 to 2009. Relative abundance is expressed in catch per unit effort (CPUE), which represents the number caught per 1-hour tow. Sampling effort represents the number of trawls that participated. The CPUE consisted of primarily adult fish, but may represent some juveniles as well.
