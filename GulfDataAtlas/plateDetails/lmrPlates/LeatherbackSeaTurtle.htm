• Leatherback Sea Turtle
    Dermochelys coriacea

    ESA Status: endangered.
    IUCN Status: vulnerable globally; Northwest Atlantic subpopulation: least concern.

    Leatherbacks are one of five out of the world's seven sea turtle species found in the Gulf of Mexico. They are the only species of the Dermochelyidae family (IUCN-MTSG, n.d.), are the largest of the sea turtles and are also the largest reptiles in the world, growing up to 2000 lbs. and 9 ft. long (NPS, n.d.). Their shells are made up of leathery, overlapping plates with seven longitudinal ridges running from head to tail. They feed on sea jellies and other soft organisms, diving to over 3000 ft. deep to forage. Leatherbacks have developed the ability to control their core body temperature despite varying external water temperatures, which extends their non-nesting range into sub-polar regions (IUCN-MTSG, n.d.).

    Sea turtle populations are impacted in multiple ways by human activities. All five of the species found in the Gulf of Mexico are currently protected under the US Endangered Species Act and the IUCN Red List of Threatened Species. However, sea turtles and their eggs are still directly harvested for food and for their shells (often illegally). They are also accidental bycatch of commercial and recreational fishing, and marine debris and pollution can kill and cause injury. Development of coastal areas- construction, armoring, dredging, and introduction of exotic vegetation- alters beach nesting and nearshore foraging habitat. Artificial lighting disorients nesting females and hatchlings (NOAA Fisheries- threats, 2014; IUCN-MTSG, n.d.). Effects of warming associated with climate change are also expected to affect critical habitats, and as the sex of hatchlings is temperature-dependent, to alter the sex ratio of populations (IUCN-MTSG, n.d.).

    Sea turtles select nesting sites based on species-specific physical conditions that are conducive to egg maturation and survival, and to hatchling dispersal into the sea (Pike, 2013). These maps show observed nesting sites and modeled nesting habitat suitability for the leatherback sea turtle in the Gulf of Mexico. These data are provided by the State of the World's Sea Turtles (SWOT) initiative (Kot et al., 2014, SWOT Reports I-IX), an international network of scientists who contribute to the only globally inclusive repository of sea turtle nesting data. The SWOT data were also used to create a map showing the sea turtle species frequency per nesting site as multiple species may nest on the same beach. The SWOT database is hosted and maintained by the Ocean Biogeographic Information System Spatial Ecological Analysis of Megavertebrate Populations (OBIS-SEAMAP, Halpin et al., 2009). These maps use the 2013 version of the SWOT database. Additional and up-to-date statistics, such as clutch size and dates, animal tracks and crawl counts can be viewed in an interactive online map of the complete SWOT database and data can be requested from the SWOT team. The habitat suitability layers are products of Maximum Entropy Modeling (MaxEnt) that identify areas of the coast where conditions for nesting grounds exist, and are ranked to show levels of nesting site suitability (Pike, 2013).
