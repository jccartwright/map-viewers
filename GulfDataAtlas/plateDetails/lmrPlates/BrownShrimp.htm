• Brown Shrimp
    Farfantepenaeus aztecus

    The commercial shrimp fishery is one of the most economically important fisheries in the Gulf of Mexico. Shrimp are also a major source of food for many forms of marine life. In U.S. waters, brown shrimp (Farfantepenaeus aztecus) range offshore of Martha's Vineyard, Massachusetts, to the Florida Keys and northward into the Gulf to the Sanibel grounds. The brown shrimp then reappear near Apalachicola Bay, and from there around the Gulf Coast to northwestern Yucatan in Mexico.

    When present in inshore waters, shrimp occur in areas with muddy or peaty bottoms rich in organic matter and decaying vegetation. Offshore, brown shrimp are most abundant on soft bottoms of mud, sand, and shell. Shrimp are omnivorous and feed on the bottom at night. Shrimp eat polychaetes, amphipods, nematodes, caridean shrimps, mysids, copepods, isopods, ostracods, mollusks, foraminiferans, chiromid larvae, and various types of organic debris.

    Brown shrimp are thought to spawn offshore beginning in early fall through the following winter. Postlarval stages move into the estuaries from February through March. Juvenile shrimp use marshes and other estuarine habitats as nursery areas and grow rapidly in waters warmer than 18°C. Subadults move out of the estuaries beginning in late spring through the hottest summer months. Adults can reach a maximum size of 7 inches and have a short lifespan (1.5 years). (NOAA's FishWatch - Brown Shrimp)

    This map shows the fisheries-independent (federal and state) trawl stations used to calculate catch per unit effort (CPUE) data for brown shrimp.

    Fisheries-Independent Data
    Federal

    These maps display brown shrimp relative abundance and sampling effort in 10-minute longitude by latitude blocks, from federal data. The information shown was obtained by summarizing 11,637 40-ft shrimp trawls taken during the Southeast Area Monitoring and Assessment Program (SEAMAP) fishery-independent Summer and Fall Shrimp/Groundfish Surveys from 1987 to 2009. Relative abundance is expressed in CPUE, which represents the number caught per 1-hour tow.

    Fisheries-Independent Data
    State
    These maps show brown shrimp mean CPUE, based on individual sampling locations as well as by water bodies, from state sources. The information displayed here shows the results of 4,285 trawls (from primarily 16-ft to 20-ft otter trawls) taken from 2000 to 2007 in estuarine and nearshore habitats. This trawl data set was obtained from the Comparative Assessment of Gulf Estuarine ecoSystems (CAGES) Relational Database, which is a compilation of estuarine fishery-independent data contributed by natural resource agencies of the Gulf States (Texas, Louisiana, Mississippi, Alabama, and Florida). Each state's gear and sampling methodologies have some differences, and CPUE values between states may not be directly comparable. CPUE is expressed in number caught per hectare.

    Fisheries-Dependent Data
    Commercial Catch
    This map displays average brown shrimp catch by grouped depth zone and grouped National Marine Fisheries Service (NMFS) statistical area (click to see depth zone - statistical area map). The four depth zones are: inshore, 0 to 10 fathoms (0 to 18 m), 10 to 30 fathoms (18 to 55 m), and >30 fathoms (>55 m); and the four subareas are comprised of the following grouped statistical zones: 1 to 9, 10 to 12, 13 to 17, and 18 to 21. These values were averaged from Gulf of Mexico shrimp landing records accumulated between 2002 and 2011. Catch refers to the amount of species taken in a defined geographic location. This differs from landings in that landings can be caught in several geographic locations, but are landed at a specific port. When species are caught, commercial fishermen record the location of the collection. When they are landed, commercial fishermen report these geographic catch locations to NMFS along with species and pounds collected.
