• Gag Grouper
    Mycteroperca microlepis

    Gag (Mycteroperca microlepis) is the most common grouper in the southeastern United States. Gag is an important part of the commercial fisheries in the South Atlantic and Gulf of Mexico. Recreational harvest is high in both areas as well. In the Gulf of Mexico, population levels of gag are below target levels. Adult gag are found from North Carolina to Brazil. The species is found in the Gulf of Mexico but not in the West Indies. Young gag inhabit estuaries from Massachusetts to Cape Canaveral, Florida, along the Atlantic coast and estuaries off west Florida, Louisiana, and Texas in the Gulf of Mexico.

    Gag are a protogynous species, maturing first as females (half of females are mature at 3.7 years old), and then becoming males (half of males mature at 10.8 years old). Primary spawning grounds in the Gulf of Mexico are along the west Florida shelf at approximately 60 to 90 m. (NOAA's FishWatch - Gag)

    Fisheries-Independent Data
    Federal

    This map shows the gag grouper data taken during the Southeast Area Monitoring and Assessment Program (SEAMAP) and National Marine Fisheries Service (NMFS) fishery-independent Reef Fish and Bottom Longline resource surveys throughout the U.S. Gulf of Mexico. These are primarily adult fish but may represent some juveniles as well.

    Gag grouper (primarily adult) relative abundance and sampling effort by 10-minute longitude by latitude blocks are summarized from 4,125 video camera sets taken during the SEAMAP Reef Fish surveys from 1993 to 2009. Abundance is expressed as minimum count and effort as the number of camera sets.

    Gag grouper (primarily adult) catch per unit effort (CPUE) and sampling effort by 15-minute longitude by latitude blocks are summarized from 1,741 bottom longline sets taken during the NMFS Bottom Longline surveys from 2000 to 2009. CPUE is expressed as the number caught per 100 hook hour and effort as the number of bottom longline sets.
