• Suitable Feeding and
    Spawning Habitats
    Atlantic Bluefin Tuna

    Atlantic bluefin tuna (ABFT) is a highly migratory species with two known main populations that mostly spawns in the Gulf of Mexico (western stock) and the Mediterranean Sea (eastern stock), and also feeds in the North Atlantic. They tolerate wide ranges of environmental conditions but appear to track changes in water temperature and currents, preferably feeding along frontal productive features (chlorophyll-a fronts) while spawning occurs within spring warming waters.

    A habitat model, developed by the European Commission Joint Research Centre's Fish Habitat Project and partner scientists (see Druon et al., 2016), has identified the potential feeding and spawning habitats of small (5-25 kg) and large (>25 kg) Atlantic bluefin tuna in the Mediterranean Sea, North Atlantic and Gulf of Mexico. The model employs an ecological niche approach that correlates the presence of the tuna, from catch and scientific survey data, with daily environmental data from satellite observations and ocean circulation models. It incorporates specific favorable environmental conditions of the fish size class, including food availability, physical tolerance to temperature, and preference to currents, that describe the spawning and feeding behaviors.

    These Gulf of Mexico seasonal composite maps generated by the habitat model show the percent daily occurrence of suitable habitat conditions for large (>25 kg) Atlantic bluefin tuna from 2003 to 2012. Spawning habitat is present from March to May, but shifts from the south-east in March-April to the north in April-May, as seen in this spawning fortnight composite map animation. Annual spawning anomaly composite maps (2003-2012) are also available. The spawning data are overlain with the boundaries of two National Marine Fisheries Service (NMFS) spring Gulf of Mexico gear restricted areas that were adopted in 2014 under Amendment 7 to the 2006 NMFS Consolidated Atlantic Highly Migratory Species (HMS) Fishery Management Plan and implemented in 2015. Feeding habitat is present throughout the autumn (October, November, December), winter (January, February, March), and spring (April, May, June) seasons and is in agreement with observation data. Feeding habitat conditions were not identified during the summer months (July, August, September). Notably, negative anomalies appear in the 2009 through 2012 annual feeding anomaly composite maps (2003 to 2012), indicating poor feeding conditions which may ultimately affect spawning activity. A feeding fortnight composite map animation is also available.

    The seasonal and large-scale mapping of suitable habitats provides important temporal and geographical information about the feeding and spawning behavior of Atlantic bluefin tuna for management. This enables resource managers to make informed decisions that can lead to more efficient fishing closure periods, better by-catch reduction, increased recruitment levels and stock recovery, thereby increasing the yield and stability of the ABFT fishery sector in the long term. For example, the daily potential preferred habitat maps can provide concrete recommendations for when and where grounds could be closed as part of a dynamic closure scheme; or the identification of the extents and duration of the habitats can improve knowledge of population distribution and movements, leading to more accurate stock assessments.
