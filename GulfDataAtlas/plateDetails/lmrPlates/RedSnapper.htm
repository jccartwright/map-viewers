• Red Snapper
    Lutjanus campechanus

    Northern red snapper are a favorite target for sport and commercial fishermen in the Gulf of Mexico. Many fish that are red are passed off as red snapper in the marketplace, but the only species that can be legally labeled red snapper is the American red snapper, Lutjanus campechanus. Red snapper have trademark red skin and red eyes and come from waters off the Southeast Atlantic and Gulf States and Mexico. Gulf red snapper was first declared overfished in 1988, and a series of management measures has been implemented since then to rebuild the stock. Currently, Gulf red snapper biomass remains very low.

    Red snapper are generally found at depths between 10 to 190 m along the eastern coast of North, Central, and northern South America and in the Gulf of Mexico. Adults live on the bottom, usually near hard structures on the continental shelf that have moderate to high relief, such as coral reefs, artificial reefs, rocks, ledges, caves, sloping soft-bottom areas, and limestone deposits. Larval red snapper swim freely within the water column. Juveniles live in shallow waters over sandy or muddy bottom habitats. They spawn from May to October, depending on their location.

    Red snapper grow at a moderate rate, up to about 40 inches (total length) and 50 pounds. They can live a long time - as much as over 50 years. Females are able to reproduce when they reach the age of 2. Red snapper feed on fish, shrimp, crab, worms, cephalopods, and some plankton. (NOAA's FishWatch - Red Snapper)

    Fisheries-Independent Data
    Federal

    This map shows the red snapper data from the larval, juvenile and adult red snapper samples routinely taken during the Southeast Area Monitoring and Assessment Program (SEAMAP) and the National Marine Fisheries Service (NMFS) fishery-independent plankton, trawl, reef fish and bottom longline resource surveys throughout the U.S. Gulf of Mexico.

    Red snapper larval relative abundance and sampling effort by 30-minute longitude by latitude blocks are summarized from 2,342 61-cm bongo net collections taken during the SEAMAP Fall Plankton surveys from 1986 to 2006. Relative abundance is expressed as the number of larvae under 10 m2 of sea surface and effort as the number of plankton tows.

    Red snapper juvenile (primarily age 0 and 1) relative abundance and sampling effort by 10-minute longitude by latitude blocks are summarized from 11,637 40-ft trawls taken during the SEAMAP Summer and Fall Shrimp/Groundfish surveys from 1987 to 2009. Relative abundance is expressed in catch per unit effort (CPUE) which represents the number caught per 1-hour tow. Sampling effort represents the number of trawls.

    Red snapper (primarily adult) relative abundance and sampling effort by-10 minute longitude by latitude blocks are summarized from 4,125 video camera sets taken during the SEAMAP Reef Fish surveys from 1993 to 2009. Relative abundance is expressed as minimum count. Sampling effort represents the number of camera sets.

    Red snapper (primarily adult) relative abundance and sampling effort by 15-minute longitude by latitude blocks are summarized from 1,741 bottom longline sets taken during the NMFS Bottom Longline surveys from 2000 to 2009. Relative abundance is expressed in catch per unit effort (CPUE) which represents the number caught per 100 hook hour. Sampling effort represents the number of bottom longline sets.

    Fisheries-Dependent Data
    Commercial Catch
    Vertical Line

    The Gulf of Mexico reef fishery consists of approximately 821 federally permitted vessels as of 2013 (SERO). Primary gears used include bottom longline and vertical line (bandit or handline). Although many reef fish species are retained, the predominant target species are groupers, Epinephelus spp., and snappers, Lutjanus spp. Fishery data are collected by mandatory observer coverage, and characteristics of this year-round fishery and sampling protocol for the Gulf of Mexico reef fish observer program are described by Scott-Denton et al. (2011). The Magnuson-Stevens Fishery Conservation and Management Act (S. 2012--109th Congress, 2005) gives NOAA authority to place observers on U.S. fishing vessels, and also requires that NOAA Fisheries maintains the confidentiality of any information obtained through compliance with it.

    This map depicts a 'hot spot' analysis of red snapper catch-per-unit-effort (CPUE) based on observer coverage of the U.S. Gulf of Mexico vertical fishery from 2007 through 2013 (28,087 sets made during 731 trips). For confidentiality purposes, data were aggregated into 15-minute longitude by latitude blocks and the average CPUE per block was calculated. If any blocks contained data from less than 3 vessels, they were removed to protect confidentiality, while not adversely affecting the results of the analysis. The 'hot spot', or Getis-Ord Gi* statistic, is used to detect clusters of high or low catch CPUE values. A statistically significant Z-score results if the sum for a block and its neighbors, when compared proportionally to the sum of all blocks in the grid, is different than what is expected. A high positive Z-score indicates a hot spot where a block with high CPUE is surrounded by neighbors with similiarly high CPUE values. A low negative Z-score indicates a cold spot, where a low-CPUE block is surrounded by blocks with similarly low values. Areas that indicate no concentration of high or low CPUE values, i.e. blocks having dissimilar values or values near the mean for the entire grid, have Z-scores near 0.
