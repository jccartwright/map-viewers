• Lane Snapper
    Lutjanus synagris

    Lane snapper (Lutjanus synagris) occur throughout the shelf area of the Gulf in depths ranging from 0 to 130 m. The species is demersal and occurs over all bottom types; however, it is most common in coral reef areas and vegetated sandy bottoms. Early and late juveniles appear to favor grass flats, reefs, and soft-bottom areas to depths of 20 m. Adults occur offshore at depths of 4 to 132 m on sand bottom, natural channels, banks, and both man-made reefs and structures. Spawning occurs in offshore waters from March through September, often in aggregations.

    Lane snapper are sexually mature at 1 year and can live up to 10 years. They are 14 inches long on average (total length), but can grow up to 23 inches and weigh up to 5 pounds. Adults prey on bottom-dwelling fish, crustaceans, and molluscs. (South Atlantic Fishery Management Council, Florida Museum of Natural History, Final Environmental Impact Statement for the Generic Essential Fish Habitat Amendment)  

    Fisheries-Independent Data
    Federal

    Lane snapper relative abundance and sampling effort by 10-minute longitude by latitude blocks are summarized from 11,637 40-ft trawls taken during the SEAMAP Summer and Fall Shrimp/Groundfish surveys from 1987 to 2009. Relative abundance is expressed in catch per unit effort (CPUE), which represents the number caught per 1-hour tow. Sampling effort represents the number of trawls. These are primarily adult fish but may represent some juveniles as well.
