• Silver Seatrout
    Cynoscion nothus

    Silver seatrout (Cynoscion nothus) are closely related to the more commonly caught sand seastrout, but silver seatrout appear to favor a slightly cooler upper temperature range (30°C) and deeper waters. Adult silver seatrout are typically offshore residents, but occasional exceptions found in inshore waters. Adults also live in higher salinities than sand seatrout and are more abundant off the Texas coast where offshore salinities tend to be higher, as opposed to the northern Gulf where sand seatrout are much more abundant. Silver seatrout spawn offshore from early May to October in Gulf of Mexico waters. Two peak periods of spawning occur: one in the spring and the other in late summer.

    Silver seatrout are commonly up to 10 inches long (total length), but can grow to a maximum of 15 inches and live up to 2 years. Adults feed on crustaceans and small fish. (Texas Parks & Wildlife Department, Species Profiles: Life Histories and Environmental Requirements of Coastal Fishes and Invertebrates (Gulf of Mexico), The Sand and Silver Seatrout Fishery of the Gulf of Mexico, United States: A Fisheries Profile)  

    Fisheries-Independent Data
    Federal

    Relative abundance and sampling effort by 10-minute longitude by latitude blocks for silver seatrout are summarized from 11,637 40-ft trawls taken during the SEAMAP Summer and Fall Shrimp/Groundfish surveys from 1987 to 2009. Relative abundance is expressed in catch per unit effort (CPUE), which represents the number caught per 1-hour tow. Sampling effort represents the number of trawls. These fish are primarily adult but may represent some juveniles as well.
