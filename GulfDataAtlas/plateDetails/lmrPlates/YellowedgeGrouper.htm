• Yellowedge Grouper
    Epinephelus flavolimbatus

    Yellowedge grouper (Epinephelus flavolimbatus) is a deep-water species found throughout the Gulf of Mexico continental shelf, and is particularly abundant in areas off Texas and west Florida. On the outer continental shelf, the species occupies high-relief hard bottoms and rocky outcrops, and is often found in association with snowy grouper and tilefish. Both adults and juveniles are also known to inhabit burrows. The species depth range is from 35 to 370 m, and adults are most common in waters deeper than 180 m. Spawning occurs from April through October and peaks in September.

    Yellowedge grouper reach sexual maturity after 5 or 6 years and can live to be over 15 years old. Adults are 20 inches long on average (total length) but can grow to a maximum of 45 inches and weigh up to 40 pounds. These groupers are protogynous hermaphrodites, meaning they function as a female first and some later transition to males. Almost all yellowedge groupers greater than 33 inches (total length) have become males, although this transition often occurs at smaller sizes. Major components of their diet comprise brachyuran crabs, fishes, and other invertebrates. (Gulf of Mexico Fishery Management Council, Texas Parks & Wildlife Department, Final Environmental Impact Statement for the Generic Essential Fish Habitat Amendment)  

    Fisheries-Independent Data
    Federal

    Relative abundance and sampling effort in 15-minute longitude by latitude blocks for yellowedge grouper (primarily adult) are summarized from 1,741 bottom longline sets taken during the NOAA Marine Fisheries Service Bottom Longline surveys from 2000 to 2009. Relative abundance is expressed in catch per unit effort (CPUE), which represents the number caught per 100-hook hour (i.e., per 100 hooks fishing for one hour). Sampling effort represents the number of bottom longline sets.
