• Pink Shrimp
    Farfantepenaeus duorarum

    This commercial shrimp fishery is one of the most economically important fisheries in the southeastern U.S. Pink shrimp (Farfantepenaeus duorarum) are found worldwide in tropical and temperate waters. Off the United States, they occur from southern Chesapeake Bay to the Florida Keys, as well as around the coast of the Gulf of Mexico to Yucatan south of Cabo Catoche. They are most abundant off southwestern Florida and the southeastern Golfo de Campeche. About 8.4 million pounds of pink shrimp were landed in U.S. fisheries in 2008, mostly off the west coast of Florida.

    Pink shrimp favor sand, shell, and mud bottoms. They bury in the substrate during the day and are active at night. Pink shrimp in the Gulf spawn multiple times during the year, peaking in warmer months from April through July. Larval/postlarval shrimp are transported into the estuaries during late spring and early summer, and young shrimp settle in brackish nursery areas containing marsh grasses. As they grow, they migrate into deeper waters and return to the ocean a few months later as adults. Females are typically larger than males and can grow to over 8 inches in total length. Maximum lifespan for pink shrimp is less than 2 years, and for harvest purposes, they are considered an annual crop, as are most shrimp species within the Gulf. Juveniles and adults are omnivorious, feeding on copepods, small mollusks, diatoms, algae, plant detritus, bacterial films, slime molds, and yeast. (NOAA's FishWatch - Pink Shrimp)

    This map shows the fisheries-independent (federal and state) trawl stations used to calculate catch per unit effort (CPUE) data for pink shrimp.

    Fisheries-Independent Data
    Federal

    These maps show pink shrimp relative abundance and sampling effort in 10-minute longitude by latitude blocks summarized from 11,637 40-ft shrimp trawls taken during the Southeast Area Monitoring and Assessment Program (SEAMAP) fishery-independent Summer and Fall Shrimp/Groundfish Surveys from 1987 to 2009. Relative abundance is expressed in catch per unit effort (CPUE), which represents the number caught per 1-hour tow. Sampling effort represents the number of trawls.

    Fisheries-Independent Data
    State
    These maps show pink shrimp mean CPUE, based on individual sampling locations as well as by water bodies, from state sources. The information displayed here shows the results of 4,287 trawls (from primarily 16-ft to 20-ft otter trawls) taken from 2000 to 2007 in estuarine and nearshore habitats. This trawl data set was obtained from the Comparative Assessment of Gulf Estuarine ecoSystems (CAGES) Relational Database, which is a compilation of estuarine fishery-independent data contributed by natural resource agencies of the Gulf States (Texas, Louisiana, Mississippi, Alabama, and Florida). Each state's gear and sampling methodologies have some differences, and CPUE values between states may not be directly comparable. CPUE is expressed in number caught per hectare.

    Fisheries-Dependent Data
    Commercial Catch
    This map displays average pink shrimp catch by grouped depth zone and grouped National Marine Fisheries Service (NMFS) statistical area (click to see depth zone - statistical area map). The four depth zones are: inshore, 0 to 10 fathoms (0 to 18 m), 10 to 30 fathoms (18 to 55 m), and >30 fathoms (>55 m); and the four subareas are comprised of the following grouped statistical zones: 1 to 9, 10 to 12, 13 to 17, and 18 to 21. These values were averaged from Gulf of Mexico shrimp landing records accumulated between 2002 and 2011. Catch refers to the amount of species taken in a defined geographic location. This differs from landings in that landings can be caught in several geographic locations, but are landed at a specific port. When species are caught, commercial fishermen record the location of the collection. When they are landed, commercial fishermen report these geographic catch locations to NMFS along with species and pounds collected.
