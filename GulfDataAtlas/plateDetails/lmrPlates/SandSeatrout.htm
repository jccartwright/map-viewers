• Sand Seatrout
    Cynoscion arenarius

    Sand seatrout (Cynoscion arenarius) are commonly found in bays, estuaries, and shallow open waters around the Gulf of Mexico. Juvenile sand seatrout prefer unvegetated benthic habitat during the summer months, and adult sand seatrout tend to migrate to deeper waters nearshore and offshore during colder months. Sand seatrout spawn in lower estuarine environments or inshore Gulf waters; their two spawning peaks are early March to May and then in August to September.

    Sand seatrout are commonly up to 14 inches in length (total length), but can grow to a maximum of 25 inches and live up to 2 years. Adults feed on shrimp and fish. (Texas Parks & Wildlife Department, Species Profiles: Life Histories and Environmental Requirements of Coastal Fishes and Invertebrates (Gulf of Mexico), The Sand and Silver Seatrout Fishery of the Gulf of Mexico, United States: A Fisheries Profile)  

    This map shows the fisheries-independent (federal and state) trawl stations used to calculate catch per unit effort (CPUE) data for sand seatrout.

    Fisheries-Independent Data
    Federal

    Relative abundance and sampling effort in 10-minute longitude by latitude blocks are summarized from 11,637 40-ft trawls taken during the SEAMAP Summer and Fall Shrimp/Groundfish surveys from 1987 to 2009. Relative abundance is expressed in catch per unit effort (CPUE), which represents the number caught per 1-hour tow. Sampling effort represents the number of trawls. These fish are primarily adult but may represent some juveniles as well.

    Fisheries-Independent Data
    State
    Using data from state sources, maps displaying sand seatrout mean catch per unit effort (CPUE) based on individual sampling locations, as well as by water bodies are available. The information shows the summarized results from 4,287 trawls (from primarily 16-ft to 20-ft otter trawls) taken from 2000 to 2007 in estuarine and nearshore habitats. This trawl data set was obtained from the Comparative Assessment of Gulf Estuarine ecoSystems (CAGES) Relational Database, which is a compilation of estuarine fishery-independent data contributed by natural resource agencies of the Gulf States (Texas, Louisiana, Mississippi, Alabama, and Florida). There are some differences in each state's gear and sampling methodologies, and CPUE values between states may not be directly comparable. CPUE is expressed in number caught per hectare.
