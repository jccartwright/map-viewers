Eastern oysters, Crassostrea virginica, in Florida

 

Oysters (Crassostrea virginica) are common in Florida estuaries where the mixing of freshwater and marine waters provide a favorable environment for productive oyster populations.  An essential characteristic of adult oysters is their ability to tolerate and thrive under wide ranges of environmental conditions, and to colonize in waters where there are favorable salinity regimes, available nutrition, and suitable substrate.  Oysters typically aggregate in clusters that over generations establish oyster reefs that are discrete structures that form distinguishable features in estuaries and coastal waters that may support viable oyster communities for decades and even hundreds of years.  The distribution and abundance of oyster populations has been described for numerous historically productive estuaries in Florida, including Suwannee Sound, Matanzas River and the Indian River, with the most abundant and productive oyster reefs occurring in Apalachicola Bay.  However, declines in oyster reefs have been exacerbated in recent years by continued coastal development and alteration of coastal freshwater drainage patterns that have strongly influenced the abundance and distribution of productive oyster reef habitat.

 

Oyster fisheries management in Florida dates back to the beginning of the 19th century. In 1913, the Florida Shellfish Commission was organized and shellfish harvesting laws were enacted; in 1933 the Florida Board of Conservation was established and assumed control of statewide shellfish management.  Since that time, various agencies [including the Florida Department of Natural Resources, the Department of Environmental Protection, the Marine Fisheries Commission, the Fish and Wildlife Conservation Commission, and the Department of Agriculture and Consumer Services] have been responsible for managing oyster resources and fisheries.  Fisheries-directed management practices have included establishing harvesting seasons, size limits, bag limits, and gear restrictions, while resource-directed management practices create and restore reef substrate.  Public management practices date back nearly a century, and the state has been involved in comprehensive oyster resource management since 1949.  Public reefs account for 90-95% of the oysters landed in Florida, so management has focused on sustaining public reefs.  Enhancing substrate by replacing cultch material (hard substrate suitable for attachment of settling oysters) has long been accepted as a beneficial management practice, and gives resource managers the opportunity to mitigate resource losses, increase production and contribute direct economic benefit to fisheries-dependent communities.  In 2009 the dockside value of reported oyster landings exceeded $7 million with an economic value greater than 5 times the dockside value.

 

Recently, oysters have been recognized as a keystone species within Florida’s estuaries, and productive oyster reef habitat as been identified as an essential component in sustaining estuarine ecosystems.  Beyond the fact that oysters and oyster reef communities represent important food sources for many species of commercially important fish and invertebrates, functioning oyster reefs are also recognized as critical structural and community components which stabilize and sustain a broad array of ecological relationships.  Sustainable reefs form the foundation for oysters, as an engineering species, to contribute to wide reaching fishery and ecological benefits.  Ecological benefits are realized through an array of ecological services in the form of increased fishery and wildlife habitat; increased biodiversity and trophic dynamics; increased filtering capacity to improve water quality and recycle nutrients; and increased structural stability to reduce coastal erosion and to protect near shore resources.  Economic benefits are also realized through harvesting, processing, and marketing fishery products and felt locally and regionally by all who enjoy high-quality, wholesome Florida seafood; depend upon commercial fisheries for employment and income; and recreate in Florida’s waters.  Because of this ecological complexity, functioning oyster communities are essential to prudent resource management.
