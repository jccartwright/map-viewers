Eastern oysters, Crassostrea virginica, in Louisiana

Range:  The estuarine environment in Louisiana spans along the coast from Mississippi Sound in St. Bernard Parish to the eastside of Sabine Pass in Cameron Parish, and is home to Louisiana’s most important mollusc species from both an economic and ecological standpoint, the American (=Eastern) oyster (Crassostrea virginica).

Harvest Season: The oyster harvest season for public oyster areas generally runs from September through April of the following year. Private oyster leases may be fished at any time provided the Louisiana Department of Health and Hospitals (LDHH) has classified the waters as open for harvest.   The LDHH may close certain areas based on water quality to protect public health.

Management: The Louisiana Department of Wildlife and Fisheries (LDWF) is responsible for oyster management on nearly 1.7 million acres of public oyster areas and also leases an additional 390,000 acres of state owned water bottoms to private entities for oyster cultivation.  For the public areas, oyster management involves the enforcement of size and possession limits, seasonal framework, and gear (dredges, tongs, and scrapers) restrictions. The planting of suitable hard substrate (cultch) on water bottoms is also a major component of oyster management on public oyster areas, along with extensive biological sampling.  Providing clean, hard settlement substrata for oyster larvae helps rehabilitate oyster reefs which have been degraded due to natural forces (i.e. hurricanes) and commercial harvest.

Economic: Louisiana traditionally produced approximately one-third of all oysters harvested in the U.S. and 50 percent of oysters harvested in the Gulf of Mexico.  Louisiana landed 14.7 million pounds of oysters with a dockside value of $49.9 million in 2009 (NMFS).  Previous economic assessments have valued this harvest at over $300 million in total economic impact to Louisiana.  Unfortunately, the annual harvest dropped to under 7 million pounds in 2010 for only the third time since 1950.

Recent Projects: Since 2007, eight reef rehabilitation projects have been constructed covering nearly 1,000 acres at a cost of approximately $9.7 million.  Additionally, LDWF conducted an oyster mortality investigation during the spring and summer of 2010 in response to an influx of low salinity waters into the estuaries of Breton Sound and Barataria Bay. For more information navigate to http://www.wlf.louisiana.gov/document/oyster-mortality-study.

Currently, LDWF and Louisiana State University have begun a cooperative project to release oyster larvae and spat in the Hackberry Public Oyster Seed Reservation as part of oyster rehabilitation efforts. For more information navigate to http://www.wlf.louisiana.gov/news/34230.  Additionally, LDWF performs an annual oyster stock assessment on the public areas each summer.  Previous years’ stock assessments can be accessed on the LDWF website at: http://www.wlf.louisiana.gov/fishing/oyster-program

Problems Encountered by the Fishery:  Since 2005, the Louisiana oyster fishery has been impacted by four major hurricanes (Katrina, Rita, Gustav, and Ike), four freshet events (2008-2011), and the largest oil spill in U.S. history (2010).  These events severely impacted both the oyster resource and the oyster industry.  Biological sampling has documented extensive oyster mortalities and incidents of failures in reproductive events, leading to significant reductions in oyster abundance on many reefs.
