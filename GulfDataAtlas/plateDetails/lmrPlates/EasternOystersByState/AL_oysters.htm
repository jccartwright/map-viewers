Eastern oysters, Crassostrea virginica, in Alabama

Geographic extent: Oysters are found throughout coastal waters of Alabama; however, significant concentrations of oysters, oyster reefs, are located in Mobile Bay and Mississippi Sound.   Over 3,000 acres of oyster reefs occur in Alabama.  Oyster reefs in Alabama are subtidal, low relief structures usually less than 2 feet in height.

Major harvesting times and economic impact:  Oysters are primarily harvested with tongs, although use of a dredge is permitted in certain areas.   For nearly five years drought conditions have persisted in Alabama which has negatively impacted the amount of oysters available for harvest.  In 2006, the last year before the drought began, over 12 million pounds of oysters were harvested in Alabama which had a dockside value of $3.6 million.   In 2010, a new oyster harvesting bill was passed in the Alabama legislature which, in part, allows the Commissioner of the Department of Conservation and Natural Resources (DCNR) to establish a closed commercial season.   Prior to 2011, oyster reefs were open to harvest year-round except when the Department of Public Health closed reefs due to temporary conditions which affect public health.  Currently, the closed season is from May 1 – September 30.  Privately leased water bottoms are used to raise oysters.  Harvest of oysters from private leases is open year round except during closures by the Department of Public Health yet landings from these areas is less than 10% of the total oyster landings under normal conditions.

Problems:  Oyster populations are under constant threat of sedimentation or damage due to tropical storms.  Hurricanes Ivan and Katrina in 2004 and 2005, respectively, levied considerable damage.    In the last five years, the Southeastern United States has been under persistent drought conditions.  These conditions have lead to increased salinities in lower Mobile Bay and much of Mississippi Sound.  These conditions were ideal for predatory oyster drills resulting in some oyster reef populations to decline 90% over the period.  In addition to reduced freshwater inputs into Mobile Bay due to the drought, upstream water use for industrial, agricultural and human needs have increased dramatically over the last 10 years.  This has exacerbated the problem of inadequate freshwater inputs.

Recovery and restoration:  For decades DCNR has enhanced oyster reefs with various cultch material including oyster shell, crushed limestone and relict freshwater clam shell.  These plantings usually occurred after hurricanes to mitigate for losses incurred from specific events.  Over the last three years, DCNR has relocated over 150 acres of oysters and planted over 300 acres of cultch.  Recently passed legislation will assist the Department of Conservation and Natural Resources with improved assessment and management of existing oyster reefs.  This will be accomplished through more precise and timely reporting of harvested oysters, increased monitoring and sampling of oyster populations, and more frequent cultch plantings.   In the last 5-7 years, various nongovernmental organizations have become more involved with oyster restoration projects.  To date these projects utilize oyster shell in conjunction with living shoreline designs.
