Eastern oysters, Crassostrea virginica, in Texas

Jennifer Pollack, PhD, Texas A&M University-Corpus Christi

Geographic extent:  Oysters occur throughout the seven bay systems in Texas, with the largest reef assemblages located in the Galveston, Matagorda, and Aransas bay systems.  Altogether, Texas bays contain an estimated 76,000 acres of oyster reef.   Oysters in Texas occur primarily on large subtidal reefs in moderate salinity regions of the bays, with vertical relief ranging from approximately 0.3 to 2 m in height.   

Major harvesting times and economic impact: Presently, oysters in Texas are commercially harvested from November 1 - April 30 in the Galveston, Matagorda, San Antonio and Aransas bay systems.  Commercial landings in 2010 exceeded 5.8 million pounds, generating over $19.2 million in revenue for the state.  Harvest from private oyster leases, located in Galveston Bay, occurs year round and accounts for approximately one quarter of the annual production and ex-vessel value.

Problems:  Oyster populations in Texas have experienced habitat loss due to historical shell dredging, overharvest, storm impacts, and disease.  In 1963, the marketable oyster size was reduced from 3.5 inches to 3.0 inches because oysters > 3.0 inches were succumbing to disease (Perkinsus marinus) before they were allowed to be harvested.  In 2005, due to concerns about overharvesting, a moratorium was placed on commercial oyster licenses.  However, in the final days before the bill was passed, there was a rush to purchase licenses, resulting in an additional 400 licenses being added to the fishery, most of which remain today.  In 2008, as a result of sediment deposition from Hurricane Ike, the Galveston Bay system lost approximately 50%, or 8,000 acres of oysters.  Most of these losses were due to sedimentation, leading to severe oyster mortalities and submerging available hard substrate, impeding larval oyster settlement and natural recovery processes.  Reduction of freshwater inflow to Texas bays is an ongoing issue, as the combination of high salinities and temperatures leads to oyster mortalities through increased disease and predation. 

Recovery and restoration:  Oyster restoration efforts have been ongoing in Texas since the late 1940s when records indicate both oyster shells and live oysters were used for reef building.  The majority of restoration projects in Texas have been constructed during the past 20 years, using primarily reclaimed shells, rock, or crushed concrete to provide hard substrates for attachment by young oysters.  Presently, oyster reef restoration projects are ongoing in the Galveston, Matagorda and Aransas Bay systems by federal, state, and NGO groups, including Texas Parks and Wildlife Department, Texas A&M University-Corpus Christi, and The Nature Conservancy.  The goal of these projects is to reverse declines in oyster populations due to overharvest, loss of substrate, and shoreline erosion.
