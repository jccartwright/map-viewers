• Blacknose Shark
    Carcharhinus acronotus

    The distribution of the blacknose shark (Carcharhinus acronotus) is limited to the western Atlantic Ocean from North Carolina south to southern Brazil, including the Caribbean Sea, Bahamas, and Gulf of Mexico. The blacknose shark is found in coastal tropical and warm temperate waters of the western Atlantic Ocean. As an inshore species, the blacknose shark resides in waters of continental shelves over sandy and coral bottoms. This species is known to form large schools, but there is segregation by size and sex. Juveniles are typically found in shallow water, and adults are located at greater depths than 9 m.

    Blacknose sharks become sexually mature by 2 years of age, and can live up to 11 years in the Gulf of Mexico. Females in the Gulf of Mexico reproduce annually, mating in May and June and giving birth the following spring to 3 to 6 pups in inshore nursery areas. Average adult size is around 4 feet (total length) with a maximum of 4.5 feet. Blacknose sharks prey on small fishes including pinfish, croaker, anchovies, etc., and schools of blacknose sharks sometimes associate with schools of mullet and anchovies. (Florida Museum of Natural History)

    Fisheries-Independent Data
    Federal

    Relative abundance and sampling effort by 15-minute longitude by latitude blocks for blacknose shark (primarily adult) are summarized from 1,741 bottom longline sets taken during the National Marine Fisheries Service (NMFS) Bottom Longline surveys from 2000 to 2009. Relative abundance is expressed in catch per unit effort (CPUE), which represents the number caught per 100-hook hour (i.e., per 100 hooks fishing for one hour). Sampling effort represents the number of bottom longline sets.
