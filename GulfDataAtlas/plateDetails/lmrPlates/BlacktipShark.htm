• Blacktip Shark
    Carcharhinus limbatus

    Blacktip sharks (Carcharhinus limbatus) are cosmopolitan in tropical to subtropical coastal, shelf, and island waters. In the Atlantic during their seasonal migration, they range from Nova Scotia to Brazil, but their center of abundance is in the Gulf of Mexico and the Caribbean Sea. The blacktip shark inhabits inshore and offshore waters, but is not a truly pelagic species. Blacktips are often seen nearshore around river mouths, bays, mangrove swamps, and in other estuaries, although they do not penetrate far into freshwater. Large aggregations of blacktips occasionally move inshore in pursuit of schooling fishes. They can frequently be found offshore and over deep waters near coral reef drop-offs, but primarily stay in the upper 30 m of the water column.

    Blacktip sharks become sexually mature between 4 and 7 years of age, and are believed to live at least 12 years. Females give birth to 1 to 10 pups in the late spring/early summer inside estuarine nursery grounds, where pups may remain for several years as they mature. Average adult size is around 5 feet (total length), but they can grow up to 9 feet and weigh over 50 pounds. Blacktips are opportunistic predators but their diet consists primarily of schooling fishes (herring, mullet, anchovy, menhaden, mackerel, etc.). (Florida Museum of Natural History, Florida Fish and Wildlife Conservation Commission)

    Fisheries-Independent Data
    Federal

    Relative abundance and sampling effort by 15-minute longitude by latitude blocks for blacktip sharks (primarily adult) are summarized from 1,741 bottom longline sets taken during the National Marine Fisheries Service (NMFS) Bottom Longline surveys from 2000 to 2009. Relative abundance is expressed in catch per unit effort (CPUE), which represents the number caught per 100-hook hour (i.e., per 100 hooks fishing for one hour). Sampling effort represents the number of bottom longline sets.
