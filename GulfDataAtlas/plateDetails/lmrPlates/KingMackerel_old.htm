• King Mackerel
    Scomberomorus cavalla

    King mackerel (Scomberomorus cavalla) travel in large schools and are caught as far north as the Gulf of Maine, but are more often from Virginia south to Brazil, including the Caribbean and the Gulf of Mexico. In the Gulf of Mexico, king mackerel have centers of distribution off south Florida and Louisiana. They are considered a coastal pelagic species and adults are found over reefs and throughout coastal waters, although they rarely enters estuaries. Migrations to the northern Gulf in the spring are believed to be temperature dependent, and the species is found in waters >20°C. While adults can be found at the shelf edge in depths to 200 m, they generally occur in waters shallower than 80 m, and at oceanic salinities from 32 to 36 ppt. The northwestern and northeastern Gulf of Mexico are considered important spawning areas, and adults spawn over the continental shelf from May to October.

    King mackerel typically grow to 3 feet but can reach a maximum of 5 feet (total length), weigh up to 100 pounds, and live more than 20 years. They are sexually mature at 2 years of age. Adults feed on other fish (including smaller mackerel), and occasionally squid and shrimp. (NOAA's FishWatch - King Mackerel, Final Environmental Impact Statement for the Generic Essential Fish Habitat Amendment)  

    Fisheries-Independent Data
    Federal

    Relative abundance and sampling effort for larval king mackerel in 30-minute longitude by latitude blocks are summarized from 2,342 61-cm bongo net collections taken during the SEAMAP Fall Plankton surveys from 1986 to 2006. Relative abundance is expressed as the number of larvae under 10 m2 of sea surface and effort as the number of plankton tows.
