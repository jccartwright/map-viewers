• Vermilion Snapper
    Rhomboplites aurorubens

    The vermilion snapper (Rhomboplites aurorubens) live in the tropical waters of the western Atlantic from Cape Hatteras to southeastern Brazil, including Bermuda, the West Indies and the Gulf of Mexico. Vermilion snapper are found throughout the shelf areas of the Gulf of Mexico, but are most common off the west coast of Florida. Their preferred habitat is irregular hard-bottom areas in waters that range in depth from 20 to 200 m, and they are most abundant in waters less than 55 m. They often form large schools. Multiple spawnings take place from April to September, peaking from June to August.

    Vermilion snapper are frequently sexually mature at 2 years, and can live 15 years or more. They grow slowly, and commonly up to 13 inches long (total length), but can reach a maximum of 24 inches and weigh 7 pounds. They feed on bottom-dwelling fish and invertebrates as well as small animals in the water column that include swimming crustaceans, cephalopods, and various zooplankton. (South Atlantic Fishery Management Council, NOAA's FishWatch - Vermilion Snapper)

    Fisheries-Independent Data
    Federal

    This map shows the vermilion snapper data from the larval and juvenile vermilion snapper samples routinely taken during the Southeast Area Monitoring and Assessment Program (SEAMAP) and the National Marine Marine Fisheries Service (NMFS) fishery-independent plankton and trawl resource surveys throughout the Gulf of Mexico.

    Relative abundance and sampling effort in 30-minute longitude by latitude blocks for larval vermilion snapper are summarized from 2,342 61-cm bongo net collections taken during the SEAMAP Fall Plankton surveys from 1986 to 2006. Relative abundance is expressed as the number of larvae under 10 m2 of sea surface. Sampling effort represents the number of plankton tows.

    Vermilion snapper (primarily juvenile, age 0 and 1) relative abundance and sampling effort in 10-minute longitude by latitude blocks are summarized from 11,637 40-ft trawls taken during the SEAMAP Summer and Fall Shrimp/Groundfish surveys from 1987 to 2009. Relative abundance is expressed as the number caught per 1-hour tow (CPUE). Sampling effort represents the number of trawls.

    Fisheries-Dependent Data
    Commercial Catch
    Vertical Line

    The Gulf of Mexico reef fishery consists of approximately 821 federally permitted vessels as of 2013 (SERO). Primary gears used include bottom longline and vertical line (bandit or handline). Although many reef fish species are retained, the predominant target species are groupers, Epinephelus spp., and snappers, Lutjanus spp. Fishery data are collected by mandatory observer coverage, and characteristics of this year-round fishery and sampling protocol for the Gulf of Mexico reef fish observer program are described by Scott-Denton et al. (2011). The Magnuson-Stevens Fishery Conservation and Management Act (S. 2012--109th Congress, 2005) gives NOAA authority to place observers on U.S. fishing vessels, and also requires that NOAA Fisheries maintains the confidentiality of any information obtained through compliance with it.

    This map depicts a 'hot spot' analysis of vermilion snapper catch-per-unit-effort (CPUE) based on observer coverage of the U.S. Gulf of Mexico vertical line fishery from 2007 through 2013 (28,087 sets made during 731 trips). For confidentiality purposes, data were aggregated into 15-minute longitude by latitude blocks and the average CPUE per block was calculated. If any blocks contained data from less than 3 vessels, they were removed to protect confidentiality, while not adversely affecting the results of the analysis. The 'hot spot', or Getis-Ord Gi* statistic, is used to detect clusters of high or low catch CPUE values. A statistically significant Z-score results if the sum for a block and its neighbors, when compared proportionally to the sum of all blocks in the grid, is different than what is expected. A high positive Z-score indicates a hot spot where a block with high CPUE is surrounded by neighbors with similiarly high CPUE values. A low negative Z-score indicates a cold spot, where a low-CPUE block is surrounded by blocks with similarly low values. Areas that indicate no concentration of high or low CPUE values, i.e. blocks having dissimilar values or values near the mean for the entire grid, have Z-scores near 0.
