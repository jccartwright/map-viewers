Abstract:
The MWANALYTE data file reports the trace concentrations of a suite of chemical contaminants in marine sediment and bivalve tissue samples collected from all U.S. coastal regions since 1986. The sediment and tissue samples were analyzed for major and trace elements and suite of organic chemical constituents, including butlytins, polynuclear aromatic hydrocarbons (PAHs), polychlorinated biphenyls (PCBs), and pesticides. The MWANALYTE file is constructed as a vertical formatted table.
Purpose:
The main objectives of the NS&T Program are the following: (1) Conduct long-term monitoring of contaminants and other environmental conditions at more than 300 sites along all U.S. coasts, (2) Advise and participate in local, regional, national, and international projects related to coastal monitoring and assessment. The objective of the sediment and tissue chemistry data file is to report the concentrations of chemical contaminants measured in NS&T marine samples. Detailed detection limits can be found in: Lauenstein, G. G. and A. Y. Cantillo (1998) Analytical Methods of the National Status and Trends Program Mussel Watch Project - 1993 -1997 Update. Attribute_Accuracy_Report:
Each batch of samples was accompanied by QC analyses consisting of method blanks, matrix spikes, matrix spike duplicates, and standard reference materials (SRMs). In total, approximately 20% of all organic analyses were QC analyses. Processing quality was considered acceptable if the following criteria were met: blanks were less than three times the minimum detection limit; accuracy, as determined by analysis of certified reference materials, was within 30% for organic analytes and within 15% for inorganic analytes; and precision, as determined by replicate analyses, was within 30% for organic analytes and within 15% for inorganic analytes. Logical_Consistency_Report:
The sampling gear does not require calibration, although it was inspected regularly for damage by mishandling or impact on rocky substrates. All sampling equipment was washed with water, and rinsed with alcohol and a final rinse with distilled water. Other solvents were used to rinse equipment earlier in the project. The analytical instruments were calibrated by standard laboratory procedures including: constructing calibration curves, running blank and spiked quality control samples, and analyzing Standard Reference Materials or Certified Reference Materials. Each batch of samples was accompanied by QC analyses consisting of method blanks, matrix spikes, matrix spike duplicates, and standard reference materials (SRMs). In total, approximately 5% of all analyses were QC analyses. Processing quality was considered acceptable if the following criteria were met: blanks were less than three times the minimum detection limit; accuracy, as determined by analysis of certified reference materials, was within 30% for organic analytes and within 15% for inorganic analytes; and precision, as determined by replicate analyses, was within 30% for organic analytes and within 15% for inorganic analytes.
Completeness_Report:
Care was taken to minimize disturbance to the sediment grabs. Grab samples that were incomplete, slumped, less than 7 cm in depth, or comprised chiefly of shelly substrates were discarded. The chance of sampling the same location was minimized by repositioning the boat (five meters downstream) after three sampling attempts. Bivalve collection criteria established for the MWP are: o Indigenous populations of mollusks must exist at a potential sampling site since the monitoring effort does not use caged mussels. o The NS&T bivalve sites coincide with historical monitoring sites (i.e., EPA Mussel Watch and California Mussel Watch) when feasible and when all other criteria are met. o The site contains indigenous bivalves of a suitable size (5 - 8 cm for mussels, 7 - 10 cm for oysters) available for collection. o The NS&T Program is not intended to quantify contaminants in hot spots. Rather, mollusk collection sites are selected to be representative of the body of water sampled. Therefore, Mussel Watch sites are not knowingly located near waste discharge points or in poorly flushed industrial waterways. o Sample substrates are limited to natural substrates or structures made of natural materials such as rock (including rip-rap and jetties), sand, or mud. Collection of samples on buoys and preserved wooden structures can yield artificially high results for some contaminants being quantified by the NS&T Program. o The site is suitable for follow-up sampling (i.e., it is not anticipated that the site will be physically disrupted by development or that the mollusk population depleted by sampling). The number of different molluscan species is kept to a minimum in order to make the greatest spatial data comparisons possible. Concentrations of metallic analytes smaller than the method detection limit were on occasion reported as null.

Process_Description:
Sediment and bivalve tissue samples were collected for the analysis of metallic and organic chemical constituents. Separate samples from the same sediment were collected for sediment grain-size analyses and on occasion Clostridum perfringens enumeration. A brief overview of the sample collection methods used by Mussel Watch field crews are described here. Subtidal bivalves and sediments were primarily collected from gasoline or diesel powered boats, 15 to 25 feet in length. Intertidal bivalves were collected by hand as were some sediment samples, with a Kynar-coated scoop. A stainless steel (coated with Kynar), Young-modified Van Veen grab sampler was typically used to collect sediments. Subsamples were taken with a Kynar coated scoop. Intertidal bivalves were collected by hand while subtidal bivalves were collected with an oyster dredge or tongs. Salinity was determined with a refractometer or a conductivity-temperature-depth probe. BIVIVALVE COLLECTION Collection methods for the bivalves may have some differences by coastal region, year, and sampling team. Details of the collection methods are found in the above stated references. An example of a sampling strategy is given. The primary objective in sampling bivalves was to obtain three discrete composite samples from three stations. When this division of the site into stations was not possible, a pool of bivalves representative of the site was collected and divided into 3 separate collections. Each collection was treated as if it were from a station. This latter procedure was usually necessary when the collection was made using a dredge, under other subtidal collecting conditions, or when the bivalve population was concentrated in a very small area. A station-sample consisted of 150 to 300 mollusks, depending on size and species. SEDIMENT COLLECTION Multiple sediment grabs (3 grabs per site and 3 grabs per each site's station were collected from each site using a Young-modifies Van Veen grab sampler. Each grab was nominally 440 cm^2 in area and up to 10 cm in depth, but only the top two-centimeter section of a grab was retained for the chemical analyses. Sample material was a composite from the three grab samples for each suit of analyses. Sediments were taken from the center of each grab, avoiding contact with the sediment grab walls. The grab was cleaned and solvent rinsed between each sampling site. Samples were taken with a Kynar coated calibrated sediment scoop After 1991 replicate collections and analyses of bivalves were only performed for newly established sites. Likewise, replicates analyses were not generally performed for sediment samples after the first 3 or 4 years of the project, except when new Mussel Watch sites were established. CHEMICAL ANALYSIS Because analytical methods change with time, methods for a particular timeframe should be checked in the appropriate methods documents. Sediment and bivalve tissues samples were analyzed for metals, butyltins, PAHs, PCBs, pesticides and periodically additional analytes. The concentrations of chemical contaminants were measured in each sample. This information is useful both in characterizing the degree of contamination at a site, as well as providing information that may indicate the origin of the contaminants. (1) Total sediment metals were analyzed by completely digesting the samples in an aggressive acid treatment, thereby releasing all metals for measurement, including metals comprising the sediment sample particles themselves. This method analyzes both the trace metals which are most toxic to estuarine organisms and the relatively non-toxic metals of crustal origin such as aluminum, iron, and silicon which may be used to estimate the natural background levels of the toxic metals in sediments (see Strobel et al., 1995). (2) Metals quantified in bivalve tissues were also performed on samples that were totally dissolved. (3) Four butyltins were measured, reflecting interest in the distribution of the anti-fouling agent tributyltin and its breakdown products. (4) Three categories of organic contaminants were measured: PAHs, PCBs, and pesticides. Twenty-four core polynuclear aromatic hydrocarbons (PAHs) were measured. An extended suite of alkylated PAHs was analyzed at selected sites in 1990-1992 and at all sites since 1993. Ancillary data such as temperature, salinity, sediment grain-size, Total Organic Carbon (TOC), and Total Inorganic Carbon (TIC) were also collected at each site. Sediment Chemistry: All analyses were performed on samples that were stored frozen. Samples analyzed for total metals were dried and completely digested in nitric/hydrofluoric/hydrochloric acids (acid persulfate for mercury). For the organic analyses, sediments were extracted using the procedures of NOAA National Status and Trends Program in Lauenstein and Cantillo (1993) and Lauenstein and Cantillo (1998). The analytical methods used were: (1) Cold vapor atomic absorption (AA) for mercury; (2) Flame AA for aluminum, iron, manganese, zinc; (3) Graphite furnace AA for arsenic, cadmium, chromium, copper, nickel, lead, selenium, silver, tin; (4) X-ray fluorescence for aluminum, arsenic, chromium, copper, iron, manganese, nickel, lead, silicone, zinc; (5) Neutron activation analysis for chromium, aluminum, iron, manganese; (6) Inductively coupled plasma atomic emission spectrometry (ICP-AES) for chromium, cadmium, nickel, lead, tin, antimony. (7) The PAHs were analyzed by gas chromatography / mass-spectrometry (GC/MS); pesticides and PCBs were analyzed by GC/ECD (electron capture detector); and the butyltins were analyzed by GC/FID (flame ionization detector). Concentrations are reported on a dry weight basis. Tissue Chemistry: All analyses were performed on samples that were stored frozen. Samples were dried and completely digested in nitric or in nitric and hydrochloric acids. For the organic analyses, tissues were extracted using the procedures of NOAA National Status and Trends Program in Lauenstein and Cantillo (1993) and Lauenstein and Cantillo (1998).
