      Abstract: 
          The ecology and population trends of secretive marsh birds are virtually unknown for the coastal marshes along 
          the Gulf of Mexico Coast. Thus, in 2005, staff at the Grand Bay National Estuarine Research Reserve 
          established a monitoring and research program to better understand the status, distribution, and habitat 
          associations of these bird species. In 2004, 40 marsh bird sampling stations were randomly located across 
          the Grand Bay NERR. Since the spring/summer of 2005, three replicate surveys have been conducted at each 
          using the standardized National Marsh Bird Monitoring Protocol. Briefly, observers broadcast the calls of 
          marsh birds to elicit response of these secretive birds. During the survey period, observers record the 
          species, call type, distance, and directions of each individual bird detected. Birds included in this 
          monitoring effort include Black Rail Least Bittern, King Rail, Clapper Rail, Common Moorhean, Purple 
          Gallinule, American Coot, Pied-billed Grebe, Marsh Wrens, Common Yellowthroat, Seaside Sparrow, Red-winged 
          Blackbird, Common Grackle, and Boat-tailed Grackle.
      Purpose: 
          The objectives of this monitoring effort at the Grand Bay NERR are (1) determine the distribution, abundance, 
          and breeding status of marsh birds, (2) determine marsh bird species-habitat relationships, and (3) document 
          short-term variations and track long-term trends in marsh bird populations.
